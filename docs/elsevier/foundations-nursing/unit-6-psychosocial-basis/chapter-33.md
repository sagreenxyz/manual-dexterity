# Chapter 33: Self-concept

## Key Terminology

- **Body image** - Perception of one's physical self
- **Identity** - One's sense of who they are
- **Identity confusion** - Uncertainty about one's sense of self
- **Role ambiguity** - Unclear expectations about one's role
- **Role conflict** - Incompatible demands or expectations between roles
- **Role overload** - Excessive role demands that exceed capacity
- **Role performance** - How one fulfills their roles
- **Role strain** - Difficulty meeting role expectations
- **Self-concept** - Thoughts and beliefs about oneself
- **Self-esteem** - Feelings and value one places on oneself
- **Sick role** - Behavioral expectations and rights of a person who is ill

## Case Study

**Mrs. Johnson**
- 52-year-old female
- Status post left radical mastectomy

  - Does not want to interact with family

- Potential self-concept issues related to body image changes

## Key Concepts

### Self-Concept
- The subjective conscious and unconscious thoughts a person has about themselves
- Encompasses both conscious awareness and deeper, unconscious beliefs about oneself
- Self-concept must be developed and maintained throughout life
- Development begins at a young age and is maintained across the lifespan

**Influences on Self-Concept:**
- Conscious and unconscious thoughts
- Adaptive development
- Cultural identity
- Coping styles
- Attitudes
- Perceptions
- Primary caregivers (influence development of self-concept and self-esteem)
  - Self-concept clarity is transmitted from parents to their children

**Physical Self-Concept:**
- Positive physical self-concept is a key component to mental health and psychological well-being
- How one perceives their physical self impacts overall mental and psychological health

### Self-Esteem
- The feelings a person has about themselves
- How they value themselves
- Influenced by self-concept

### Relationship Between Self-Concept and Self-Esteem
- Self-concept influences self-esteem
- A person's thoughts about themselves affect their feelings about themselves
- The cognitive component (self-concept) shapes the emotional/evaluative component (self-esteem)

### Health Problems
- Health problems threaten self-concept and self-esteem
- Illness can challenge one's sense of self and self-worth

### Development Stages
- Psychosocial and cognitive development stages influence self-concept and self-esteem
- These stages affect:
  - How self-concept adjusts over time
  - How self-esteem is maintained throughout development

### Cultural Considerations
- Cultural factors can affect self-concept and self-esteem
- Cultural background influences how individuals perceive and value themselves

## Problems in Self-Concept

Common self-concept problems include:
- **Disturbed body image** - Altered perception of one's physical self
- **Altered self-concept** - Changes in how one thinks about themselves
- **Low self-esteem** - Diminished feelings of self-worth
- **Role conflict** - Inconsistency or difficulty with perceived social roles

## Nursing Role

- The nurse can help the patient adapt and adjust to alterations in self-concept
- Nursing interventions support patients through changes in self-perception and self-worth
- The nurse can help the patient develop coping strategies to improve quality of life and health outcomes
